The Core Principle

A Rwandan mining cooperative or trading company cannot access cheap financing today not because lenders are unwilling — but because neither party can accurately measure the value or risk of the assets being offered as collateral. This system solves that problem at source: measure first, value second, tokenise third, finance fourth. Each layer builds on the one below it, and each layer makes financing cheaper, faster, and more adapted to the economic realities of each entity type.

Layer 1 — Financial Intelligence

Measuring What Matters: Revenue, Costs & Benchmarks

Layer 1 produces the financial signals that feed every other layer. Without accurate cost structures and inventory valuations at company level, nothing above it can function.

📊
1A
Revenue Model & Cost Structure
Build the complete P&L of any mineral sector company — revenues, variable costs, fixed costs, breakeven point. Identify where margin is made and where it leaks.
▼ EXPAND
Revenue per tonne exported
Rev = Volume × (Pmkt − TC − RC − QD − LC)
Pmkt: LME/LBMA benchmark · TC: Treatment Charges · RC: Refining Charges · QD: Quality Deductions · LC: Logistics Cost
Gross Margin
GM = Rev − VC
VC = Energy + Labour + Consumables + Royalties
Variable costs scale with production volume. Royalties typically 4–6% of export value in Rwanda.
Net Operating Margin
NOM = GM − FC
FC = Depreciation + Admin + Financing costs
Fixed costs do not scale with volume — critical for breakeven calculation.
Breakeven Volume (MTU)
BEV = FC ÷ (Pmkt − TC − RC − VC/unit)
MTU = Metric Tonne Unit. Below BEV, the company destroys value with every tonne produced.
Prefinancing Discount Cost
PDC = Volume × Pmkt × d%
d% = prefinancing discount rate (typically 12–18% of benchmark price). PDC is the hidden transfer to international trading houses — quantified for the first time through this system.
Cost Structure by Category
Energy: ~35%
Labour: ~28%
Consumables: ~18%
Royalties & taxes: ~12%
Admin & financing: ~7%
Indicative structure for semi-industrial mining — varies significantly by entity type

Layer 3 — Financing Instruments

Five Instruments: Diversified, Transparent & Competitively Priced Financing

The core problem is not prefinancing itself — it is foreign prefinancing dependency that hands international traders disproportionate bargaining power to set prices arbitrarily below market benchmarks, effectively charging an opaque and very high financing cost. The answer is not to eliminate prefinancing — it is to give Rwandan mineral companies access to a diversified portfolio of financing instruments, locally and internationally, at transparent and competitive rates. Data-driven financial intelligence is what makes this choice possible: only when a company knows its own cost structure, asset values, and risk profile can it identify the cheapest financing option available to it and negotiate from a position of knowledge.

The Decision Framework

For each financing decision, the RMB-FinIntel analytics engine compares: (1) the effective cost of each available instrument (advance rate, interest, fees, price discount if any); (2) the cycle duration matched to the asset being financed; (3) the collateral quality score from Layer 2; (4) the company's current Financing Cost Index (FCI). The output is a ranked recommendation — with the cheapest, most adapted instrument at the top. The company decides. The system informs.

Interactive Navigator — Balance Sheet & Financing Structures

Mining Entities: Assets vs Financing Profile

Balance sheet structures are not the same across mineral sector entities — and therefore neither are their financing needs or optimal financing models. A processing company with heavy machinery has fundamentally different collateral and financing cycles than a trading company dominated by receivables and finished goods. Understanding these structural differences at company level — and aggregating them at national level — is what allows Rwanda to map its mineral sector financing needs precisely and engage investors with concrete, evidence-based proposals.

🏢 One Company, Multiple Entities

Within a single mining company, there may be several distinct entity types operating simultaneously — a cooperative-style extraction unit, a processing subsidiary, and a trading arm. Each carries its own balance sheet, financing needs, and risk profile. The financial intelligence framework must capture all layers.

🔑 Asset Ownership Models

For heavy assets not used at 100% capacity by a single entity, alternative ownership models deserve exploration: an external asset-owning entity that leases equipment to cooperatives; shared ownership cooperatives; public-private equipment pools. These models change the balance sheet structure and open new financing possibilities — and their financial viability can only be assessed through company-level financial intelligence.

📊 National Aggregation

Automated tools in the RMB-FinIntel platform will aggregate individual balance sheets into a national-level financing needs map — showing total sector asset base, total financing gap by instrument type, and priority intervention points. This gives Rwanda a powerful evidence base for investor negotiations and policy design.

The financing equilibrium rule applies across all entities: long-term assets must be financed by long-term sources (equity + long-term borrowings). Mismatches — financing long-term assets with short-term borrowings — create structural fragility. The financial intelligence framework detects and flags these mismatches automatically.

⚠️ Current Reality — The Structural Problem This Programme Must Address

Across all entity types in Rwanda's mineral sector, a common and critical structural imbalance exists: Short Term borrowings dominate the financing structure (70–90%), while Long Term assets represent a significant share of the balance sheet. This mismatch is not a choice — it is a consequence of the absence of acceptable collateral and accurate asset valuation.

The consequences are severe: financing costs are extremely high (20–30% for artisanal miners, 15–18% for other entities), equity is suppressed (companies cannot accumulate profits when financing costs eat margins), and Rwandan companies negotiate with financiers from a position of weakness — accepting unfavourable terms because they have no alternatives.

The programme's ambition: by building accurate financial intelligence, valuing assets at their true market value, and enabling tokenisation of collateral, the RMB-FinIntel programme progressively rebalances this structure — reducing Short Term borrowing, lowering financing costs, and enabling companies to accumulate equity. As they grow financially stronger, they negotiate cheaper financing — creating a virtuous cycle that strengthens Rwanda's entire mineral sector.
